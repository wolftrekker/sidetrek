So what's the deal
with Sidetrek?

Standard GeoGuessr maps point the camera down the road. That's just how it's always been done, and it works. But it also means a lot of rounds play out the same way: you're reading the road markings, the signage, the Google car blur, and you've already got a shortlist before you've even looked at what's around you.

Sidetrek adds new dimensions to the default. Zoom in a little and the Google car disappears from view — no antenna, no car roof, not even a car blur. Pan away from the road sometimes and suddenly you're looking at a valley, a river bend, a crumbling house, the kind of angle a passenger might take if they bothered to look out the window.

It adds flavour to NMPZ that doesn't come up much: you're reading the world, not just the road.

No car metas

Every location is slightly zoomed in so the Google car doesn't show. No roof rack. No antenna. The first thing you're reading is the actual world around you, not the Google car you're perched on top of.

Hybrid panning

A good chunk of Sidetrek goes beyond road-facing. Locations are panned toward whatever's actually worth looking at — valleys, rivers, farmland, architecture.

Where does Sidetrek take you?

Distribution is carefully balanced to make the map as fun and varied as possible. Larger countries get a healthy share of locations to reflect their regional diversity, while smaller or limited-coverage countries are kept more modest — so no area of the map feels overplayed.

GeoGuessr maps are capped at around 111,250 locations. Sidetrek draws from a pool of over 135,000 — so each time the map updates, a fresh selection is pulled from that pool, keeping things varied over time.

What's excluded

Some low-quality coverage is deliberately left out to keep the map quality consistent: Gen 1 (oldest potato cam), Shitcam/Titancam in Europe, and Titancam coverage from Nigeria (between 2023–2025), Lebanon, and São Tomé and Príncipe.
