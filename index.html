So what's the deal
with Sidetrek?

Standard GeoGuessr maps point the camera down the road. That's just how it's always been done — and it works. But it also means a lot of rounds play out the same way: you're reading the car, the antenna, the road markings, and you've already got a shortlist before you've even looked at what's around you.

Sidetrek flips the default. Zoom in a little and the Google car disappears from view — no antenna, no car roof, not even a car blur. Pan away from the road sometimes and suddenly you're looking at a valley, a river bend, a crumbling wall, the kind of angle a passenger might take if they bothered to look out the window.

It adds a dimension to NMPZ that doesn't come up much: you're not just reading metadata, you're actually reading the world.

No car metas

Every location is slightly zoomed in so the Google car doesn't show. No car roof. No antenna. The first thing you're reading is the actual world around you, not the camera rig you're sitting in.

Hybrid panning

A lot of Sidetrek goes beyond road-facing. Locations are panned toward whatever's actually worth looking at — valleys, rivers, farmland, architecture. Each creative angle is locked to a specific Street View snapshot, so the panning choices don't disappear when Google updates its imagery.

Where does Sidetrek take you?

The distribution here is deliberate. Rather than filling the map proportionally by landmass or Street View coverage, locations were chosen to maximise variety — representing all regions of the world fairly, while leaning toward places that are visually interesting or underrepresented in other maps. No two rounds should feel like the same vibe.

GeoGuessr maps are capped at around 111,250 locations. Sidetrek draws from a pool of over 130,000 — so each time the map updates, a fresh selection is pulled from that pool, keeping things varied over time.
